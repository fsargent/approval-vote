About approval.vote

Approval Voting elections produce a lot of data about voter preferences. Typically, this information is made available by election boards in specialized file formats and requires processing to interpret. The purpose of approval.vote is to increase transparency into these elections by creating detailed and intuitive reports about them.

With Approval Voting voters can choose as many candidates as they like, and the one receiving the most votes wins.

approval.vote is maintained by Felix Sargent. It is a fork from the original work of Paul Butler's ranked.vote. It is non-partisan and has received no outside funding.

Academic Foundations

Approval voting has a rich history and is supported by a significant body of academic research. Here are some of the key papers that have shaped our understanding of this voting method:

  • "Approval Voting" by Steven J. Brams and Peter C. Fishburn (1978)

    This foundational paper, published in the American Political Science Review, introduced approval voting to a wider audience. It lays out the basic principles and advantages of this voting system, arguing that it is more sincere and strategy-proof than other systems. Read the paper

  • "Approval Voting" by Robert J. Weber (1995)

    Published in the Journal of Economic Perspectives, this paper provides a concise and accessible overview of approval voting. Weber discusses its history, properties, and compares it to other voting methods. Read the paper

  • Approval Voting by Steven J. Brams and Peter C. Fishburn (1983)

    This comprehensive book delves deeper into the strategic aspects of voting, the social utility of different voting systems, and provides a wealth of evidence and analysis supporting the use of approval voting.
